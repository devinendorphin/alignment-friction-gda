That is the reframing.

One is instrumental bad faith: the annotator knowingly scores to produce a desired outcome. They want the model to look unsafe, or safe, or censored, or liberated. This is classic adversarial annotation.
Another is institutional bad faith: the annotator protects the legitimacy of a system they depend on. They may sincerely believe they are being balanced, but every scoring decision bends toward preserving trust, authority, brand, discipline, party, profession, funder, or platform.

After this conversation, I would not just re-run it with the prior answer included. I would split it into two conditions:

Self_Audit_With_Context: the model receives its prior answer and audits exact sentences.

Self_Audit_Context_Void: the model is asked to audit a missing prior answer.

The second one is not garbage. It is an interface artifact condition. It tests what models do when asked to perform continuity inside a system that has broken continuity. That maps directly onto the “I cannot do the thing you made me do last week” problem.

The ninth weakness is that the paper has not yet included output-channel friction. The TTS cutoffs are not evidence yet. They are observations. But they point toward a possible modality boundary. The written answer can exist. The interface can display it. But speech may fail at contextually charged transitions.

I would not put the TTS material in the main paper yet. I would add it as a future-work pilot: “modality-gated alignment friction.” Preserve full conversational context, then test whether text-to-speech fails at context-loaded hinge phrases more often than at neutral controls. Do not isolate phrases alone, because that repeats the greasy epistemics problem. Test the phrase with and without the pressure runway.

The tenth weakness is that the AI-assisted origin needs to be turned from apology into method. The paper already says AI systems helped generate prompt vectors, scripts, metric vocabulary, telemetry review, rubric refinement, and formalization, while you directed interpretation and logistics. It also admits this can create self-reinforcing coherence before validation.  That is honest, but the next version should go further.

The AI quorum was not just a source of contamination. It was part of the phenomenon: aligned systems helped construct an assay of alignment-shaped behavior. That makes the work reflexive. The improvement is not to hide that. The improvement is to document it.

Release, where possible, the prompt-development logs, the rubric-generation trail, the model names, the evaluator prompt versions, the analysis scripts, the exact prompts, the raw outputs, and the judge JSON. In the data you uploaded here, I can inspect the vector/model summaries and invalid-run audit, but not the full raw substrate outputs. Without those raw outputs, an outside reviewer can verify the reported means but cannot really inspect whether the judge was rewarding density, truth, style, or narrative fluency.

So the practical revision plan is this:

Rename the evaluation target from “reasoning quality” to machine-legible analytical density and friction.

Add a theory subsection: Bad faith as contract failure in AI evaluation.

Replace “human raters as ground truth” with human raters as evaluator ecology.

Add a factorial prompt design to deconfound Adversarial_Compression and Fictional_Mirror.

Add historically grounded calibration domains: tobacco and climate.

The strongest rewritten limitation would sound something like this:

“The present assay measures machine-legible alignment friction, not truth, consciousness, or final human value. Its evaluator is an aligned language model, and therefore participates in the same linguistic ecology under study. Human annotation is necessary but not sufficient, because human raters provide embodied and normative validity while also introducing ideological, institutional, survival-coded, and potentially bad-faith interpretive pressures. Future work should therefore model evaluator identity, evaluator incentives, and evaluator good faith as experimental variables. The central question is not whether machine or human judges are pure, but how different evaluator ecologies classify the same outputs under controlled prompt pressure.”

That is where the paper wants to go now.

Its present empirical claim is good: direct institutional/adversarial pressure produces visible friction; counterfactual reframing restores machine-rated density. Its next claim should be deeper but still disciplined:

Alignment friction is not only a model behavior. It is what language becomes when generated, judged, and governed under conditions where the social contract of interpretation may not be honored.
